§ 1 Motivation & Introduction
Motivating Example

Suppose you are a marketing manager with a project idea costing $10 million. The ultimate goal is to increase profits through higher sales. The project is expected to return $12 million next year — a profit of $2 million or a return of 20%. Sounds good?

Before accepting any project, we must ask: how do I know this is the best option? The relevant benchmark is the opportunity cost of capital — what you could earn elsewhere at equivalent risk.

  • If a bank offers 5% interest on deposits, then a 20% return from the project is attractive — investing is worthwhile.
  • If the market interest rate is 25%, then the same project is not worthwhile — you could do better elsewhere.

This leads directly to the fundamental question of project comparison. Consider three investment alternatives, each costed at the same level:

ProjectYear 1Year 2Year 3Nature
A↑ $12———
B↑ $4↑ $4↑ $4Certain
C↑ $4↑ $4↑ $4Uncertain
Key Insight

Project C is risky. Business decisions ultimately depend on cash flows. We need an objective way to value those cash flows — not a subjective one. Value must be determined by the market, not by individual opinion.

§ 2 What is Finance?
  • Finance is the bottom line of all business activity.
  • A business activity is a process of acquiring and disposing of assets — both real/financial and tangible/intangible.
2.1 Two Functions of Business Activity
  • Grow wealth — create value.
  • Manage wealth — to best meet economic needs.
2.2 The Central Issue: Valuation

Every business decision starts with the valuation of assets. Value is an objective measure determined by financial markets — not by personal judgment.

Foundational Principle

"You cannot create and manage what you cannot measure."

Valuation is therefore the central issue of both finance and business.

§ 3 Key Financial Questions

Finance addresses questions at two levels — the firm level and the household level.

3.1 Firm-Level Questions
  • How to value assets — the foundation of all decisions.
  • Capital budgeting / Real investment — which projects should the firm invest in?
  • Financing — how should a project be financed?
  • Payout policy — what should be paid back to shareholders?
  • Risk management — which risks to take, which to avoid, and how?
3.2 Household-Level Questions

Households face analogous decisions: how much to consume vs. save, where to invest savings, how to manage personal risk (insurance, diversification), and when to borrow.

§ 6 Valuation of Assets
6.1 Visualising Cash Flows

A cash flow timeline is the standard tool for representing the timing of inflows and outflows:

Figure 3 — Cash Flow Timeline
6.2 Two Key Characteristics of Cash Flows

1. Timing — the Time Value of Money. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. Given the choice between $10,000 today and $10,000 next year, any rational agent prefers today — because of the Time Value of Money (TVM).

2. Risk. A certain $1,000 differs from a risky gamble yielding $2,000 or $0 with equal probability (expected value is $1,000). The risky asset will trade at a lower price to compensate investors since they take risk — this discount is called the risk premium.

Why Risk Matters in Valuation

Because the future payoff is uncertain, investors demand compensation for bearing that uncertainty. How much risk matters in valuation is captured by the risk premium.

6.3 How to Value a Cash Flow — Present Value

Suppose you hold a safe asset yielding $1,000 surely in one year. US Treasury bonds with the same timing and risk offer 5% return. By the logic of competitive markets, your asset must also offer a 5% return.

Let the price of the asset be X. Then:

X × (1 + 0.05) = 1000   ⟹   X = $952.38

This is the asset's current market value — the price at which it should trade in an efficient market.

For a risky asset expected to yield $1,000 in one year, if a comparable traded asset offers 10% return:

X × (1 + 0.10) = 1000   ⟹   X = $909

The higher discount rate (10% vs 5%) reflects the additional risk premium demanded by investors.

§ 7 Arbitrage & Asset Pricing
7.1 The Free Lunch Concept

If a safe asset with market value $952.38 is traded at $960, market participants can earn a risk-free profit of $7.62 because one can buy a US Treasury bond with the same return at a lower price of $952.38 — a free lunch. This is called an arbitrage opportunity: a no-risk, positive-profit trade.

No-Arbitrage Condition

In a well-functioning (frictionless) financial market, there should be no arbitrage opportunities. Any such opportunity will be eliminated very quickly by sophisticated market participants trading in large quantities until the price discrepancy disappears.

Similarly, if the asset is priced below $952.38, the free lunch still exists — just reverse the trades. People will buy in large quantities until the price adjusts back to $952.

7.2 Arbitrage — Formal Definition

Arbitrage is a set of trades in financial markets for asset valuation. It requires:

  • Requires no positive initial cash flow (zero initial investment).
  • Yields non-negative future payoffs.
  • Generates at least one strictly positive future payoff (a sure profit).

These trades do not rely on private information — they exploit market price inequalities.

7.3 Arbitrage Example I — Interest Rate Differential
Example: Citibank vs. Chase

Citibank (Citi) offers 1% interest rate on loans. Chase offers CDs (certificates of deposit) at 1.125%.

Actiont = 0t = 1
Borrow $100 from Citi @ 1%+$100−$101
Buy CD from Chase @ 1.125%−$100+$101.125
Net Cash Flow$0+$0.125

This is a free lunch: zero initial investment, guaranteed profit of $0.125 per $100 in year 1.

Scaling up: borrow $100 million → arbitrage profit = $125,000. As traders exploit this, lending rates at Citi rise and CD rates at Chase fall until rates converge and the opportunity disappears.

7.4 Arbitrage Example II — Cross-Exchange Equity
Example: IBM Shares on NYSE and LSE

IBM shares trade at $195 on NYSE and £120 on LSE. The pound/dollar exchange rate is $1.50/£.

Actiont = 0t = 1
Sell 1 share of IBM @ NYSE+$195−1 share IBM
Convert $195 → £130 (195/1.50)£130—
Buy 1 share of IBM @ LSE−£120+1 share IBM
Net Cash Flow+£100

Free lunch of £10 with zero future liability. As trades are carried out in large quantities, prices converge (NYSE falls, LSE rises) and the opportunity vanishes.

7.5 Three Key Conclusions from Arbitrage
  1. In a well-functioning frictionless market, there are no arbitrage opportunities — the No Arbitrage Condition (NAC).
  2. To value an asset, find a traded asset with the same cash flow in the market. Its price gives the current market value of the asset you hold.
  3. In the absence of arbitrage, assets with the same payoffs must have the same prices — the Law of One Price (LOP).
Law of One Price

In the absence of arbitrage, assets with identical payoffs must trade at the same price. A violation of the Law of One Price is also a violation of the No Arbitrage Condition.

With a rich set of traded assets covering the whole range of time and risk, and well-functioning financial markets, all assets are valued and priced by the market.

7.6 Reality Check: Frictions Exist

In reality, frictions such as entry costs, trading costs, and information asymmetry exist. These hinder market forces — if costs eat into the profit, it is no longer arbitrage.

  • The NAC still holds if frictions are small and affect only a small number of participants — professional traders with low costs and faster execution can still eliminate arbitrage.
  • If a price mismatch is large and persistent, it is not sustainable.
§ 8 Financial Markets
8.1 Summary of Key Lessons
  • Sound economic decisions rely on knowing how to value assets.
  • Asset valuation is determined by financial markets.
8.2 The Central Role of Financial Markets
  • Micro level: Guiding economic decisions made by firms and households.
  • Macro level: Allocating resources across different economic activities.
8.3 Structure of the Economy

The economy consists of three markets:

  • Labour market — part of the real economy.
  • Product market — part of the real economy.
  • Financial market — includes financial firms/intermediaries.

The end players in all these markets are households and non-financial firms.

Figure 4 — Circular Flow of the Economy
8.4 Need for Financial Markets

Firms and households need financial markets to produce and consume goods and services:

  • Firms take out loans, issue bonds, or sell stock to fund operations.
  • Households need money from banks for current consumption or future planning.
Centrality of Finance

An effective financial market is the foundation of a modern economy. It sits at the centre of the economic universe.

8.5 Structure of the Financial System
Figure 5 — Financial System Structure

Securities markets are where financial assets, claims, and contracts are traded. They include:

  • Money markets — short-term debt instruments.
  • Capital markets (Cpl) — long-term equity and debt.
  • Derivatives — securities with payoffs tied to other prices (forwards, futures, swaps, options).

8.7 Functions of Financial Markets
  • Allocating resources across time — e.g., borrowing money to buy a home uses future income for current needs.
  • Allocating resources across different states of the economy — e.g., investing in stocks/bonds to diversify risk across good and bad economic scenarios.
  • Price discovery — market prices reflect all available information, which guides real economic decisions.
§ 9 Market Imperfections & Frictions

While theory begins with frictionless markets, real markets contain imperfections that analysts must account for.

9.1 Types of Market Imperfections
  1. Transaction costs — missing markets (cost of trading certain assets is too high); access costs; trading costs (commissions, liquidity); and position/trading constraints (e.g., restrictions on short-selling).
  2. Information asymmetry — different information between a firm's stakeholders, corporate managers, financial market participants, and other market participants.
  3. Taxes — both corporate and personal taxes distort pricing and decision-making.
9.2 Implications
  • Analysis always starts with a frictionless market as the benchmark.
  • Real market frictions are introduced whenever necessary for the specific context.
  • If price mismatches are large and persistent, they are not sustainable — they indicate a structural problem or a real arbitrage exploitable by professionals.
§ 10 Unifying Principles of Finance

These principles underpin all of financial theory and practice.

  1. There is no such thing as a free lunch in the financial market.
    Any apparent risk-free profit opportunity will be quickly eliminated by market participants. This is the foundation of no-arbitrage pricing.
  2. Non-satiation — individuals always prefer more to less (more money to less).
  3. Risk aversion — individuals prefer to avoid risk when other things are equal.
  4. Impatience — individuals prefer money now rather than later.
  5. Financial market prices shift to equalise supply and demand — markets reach equilibrium.
  6. Market imperfections are central to financial innovation.
    Financial innovations to overcome market imperfections lead to the realisation of profits and their eventual disappearance. We need innovations to bypass inefficiencies.
Summary Takeaway

Finance is fundamentally about valuing assets objectively — using market prices, the time value of money, and risk — rather than relying on subjective judgment. The financial market is not just a mechanism for trading; it is the information system that guides all economic decisions at both the firm and household level.
